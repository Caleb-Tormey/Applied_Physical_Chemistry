## 12.3 Zeroth-Order Reactions: The Saturated System

In a zeroth-order reaction, the rate is completely independent of the concentration of the reactant. 
$$\text{Rate} = k[\text{A}]^0 = k$$

Because any number to the power of zero is 1, the rate is just a flat constant. If you double the concentration of the reactant in the beaker, the reaction does not speed up. The integrated rate law is simply:
$$[\text{A}]_t = -kt + [\text{A}]_0$$

### The Physical Reality of Zero-Order

How is it physically possible to add more reactants to a system without speeding up the collisions? 

Consider the sublimation of a solid block of dry ice ($CO_2$). The molecules escaping into the gas phase can only leave from the *exposed surface area* of the block. The millions of $CO_2$ molecules buried deep inside the interior of the crystal lattice cannot react; they must wait in line for the molecules above them to leave first. If you add more mass to the interior of the block, you have increased the total concentration of $CO_2$, but the surface area remains the same. The rate of sublimation is flat.

**Biochemical Relevance: The Liver's Limit**
In biochemistry, zeroth-order kinetics occur when a system is completely **saturated**. 

The human liver clears ethanol (alcohol) from the bloodstream using the enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase. If you drink a massive amount of alcohol, every single active site of every single enzyme in your liver is instantly filled. The enzymes are working at their absolute maximum physical speed. 

At this point, the clearance of alcohol becomes zeroth-order. Adding more alcohol to your bloodstream will not speed up the enzymes; it just forces the excess alcohol molecules to "wait in line" in your blood, exactly like the molecules buried inside the dry ice block. Because the rate of clearance is a flat constant (roughly 1 standard drink per hour), alcohol clearance yields a perfectly straight line on a standard concentration vs. time graph, rather than the curved exponential decay seen in first-order drugs like caffeine or ibuprofen.
