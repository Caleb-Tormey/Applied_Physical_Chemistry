## 13.5 Enzyme Inhibition

Pharmaceutical drugs and cellular toxins generally work by binding to enzymes and shutting them down. The Lineweaver-Burk plot is the ultimate diagnostic tool for figuring out *how* a drug works. 

### Competitive Inhibition
**The Physical Reality:** The drug looks identical to the natural substrate. It competes for the exact same active site on the enzyme. 

**The Mathematical Result:** Because the drug is blocking the active site, the enzyme's apparent affinity for the actual substrate drops (Apparent $K_m$ **increases**). However, if you flood the beaker with a massive, overwhelming amount of substrate, you can statistically outcompete the drug and still reach the original $V_{max}$. 
* *Lineweaver-Burk Signature:* The lines intersect perfectly at the y-intercept. 

### Uncompetitive Inhibition
**The Physical Reality:** The drug does *not* bind to the free enzyme. It only binds to the $ES$ complex, locking the substrate inside the enzyme like a vault and preventing catalysis. 

**The Mathematical Result:** Because it locks the $ES$ complex together, it artificially looks like the enzyme is binding the substrate *tighter* (Apparent $K_m$ **decreases**). But because a percentage of the enzymes are permanently taken out of commission, the physical speed limit drops (Apparent $V_{max}$ **decreases**). 
* *Lineweaver-Burk Signature:* The lines are perfectly parallel. 

### Noncompetitive (Mixed) Inhibition
**The Physical Reality:** The drug binds to an allosteric (distant) site on the enzyme. It doesn't care if the substrate is bound or not. It physically warps the 3D shape of the enzyme so it can no longer perform catalysis. 

**The Mathematical Result:** The substrate can still bind normally ($K_m$ is **unchanged**), but the active site is broken, meaning the total working concentration of the enzyme is reduced ($V_{max}$ **decreases**). 
* *Lineweaver-Burk Signature:* The lines intersect exactly on the x-axis.
