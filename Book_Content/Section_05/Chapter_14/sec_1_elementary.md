## 14.1 Elementary Reactions and Molecularity

The most fundamental rule of kinetics is that a complex biochemical reaction almost never happens in a single, massive collision. If a balanced chemical equation has four reactants, they do not all miraculously smash into each other at the exact same millisecond with perfect 3D geometry. 

Instead, the overall reaction is broken down into a series of simple, bite-sized steps called **elementary reactions**. 

### Molecularity
Because an elementary reaction represents a *single, literal collision event* in the beaker, we can actually write its rate law directly from its stoichiometry (something we explicitly forbade for overall reactions in Chapter 11). We classify these steps by their **molecularity**—the number of molecules involved in the collision:

* **Unimolecular:** A single molecule shakes itself apart or undergoes a structural rearrangement. ($A \rightarrow P$). Rate law: $\text{Rate} = k[A]$
* **Bimolecular:** Two molecules collide. ($A + B \rightarrow P$). Rate law: $\text{Rate} = k[A][B]$
* **Termolecular:** Three molecules perfectly collide at the exact same time. This is statistically incredibly rare in biology and is almost universally avoided by cellular mechanisms.

### The Rate-Determining Step (RDS)
Imagine a three-lane highway that suddenly merges into a single dirt road before opening back up to three lanes. The speed of traffic is entirely dictated by that single, narrow dirt road. In a multi-step mechanism, the elementary step with the highest activation energy ($E_a$) is the slowest. This **Rate-Determining Step (RDS)** acts as the kinetic bottleneck. The overall reaction can absolutely never go faster than its RDS.
