## 6.4 Van der Waals Forces II: Induced Dipoles (Debye and London)

We just saw how molecules with permanent partial charges (dipoles) attract each other. But what if a molecule doesn't have a permanent dipole? What if it is completely nonpolar, like a molecule of oxygen ($O_2$) or a long hydrocarbon lipid tail? 

Do nonpolar molecules experience intermolecular forces? Absolutely. If they didn't, it would be physically impossible to liquefy nitrogen or freeze carbon dioxide into dry ice. The forces that hold nonpolar molecules together are driven by **induced dipoles**.

### Dipole-Induced Dipole (Debye Forces)

Imagine dropping a perfectly nonpolar molecule, like $O_2$, into a beaker of water. Water has a strong permanent dipole. As a water molecule bumps into the $O_2$, the partial negative charge on the water's oxygen atom physically repels the electron cloud of the $O_2$ molecule. 

This forces the $O_2$ electron cloud to shift, temporarily creating a "squished" partial positive end and a partial negative end. The water has **induced** a dipole in the $O_2$. Now that $O_2$ temporarily has partial charges, the two molecules attract! 

How easily a nonpolar molecule can be squished is dictated entirely by its **polarizability** ($\alpha$). 
* *Link back to Chapter 5:* Remember our Raman spectroscopy selection rules? We saw that larger, looser electron clouds are much more polarizable than small, tight ones. 

Therefore, a large atom like Iodine is incredibly polarizable and forms strong induced dipoles, while a tiny atom like Helium is tightly held and barely polarizable at all. Like Keesom forces, the interaction energy of these Debye forces drops off at a rate of **$1/r^6$**.

**Biochemical Relevance: Dissolved Oxygen**
Without Debye forces, fish would drown. Oxygen ($O_2$) is completely nonpolar, yet it must dissolve into the watery environments of blood and oceans to sustain life. It is the permanent dipole of water inducing a temporary dipole in the $O_2$ that provides enough attractive energy to keep a small amount of oxygen dissolved in the aqueous phase!

### Instantaneous Dipole-Induced Dipole (London Dispersion Forces)

Now for the ultimate test: how do two completely nonpolar molecules attract *each other*? There are no permanent dipoles around to do the squishing. 

To answer this, we must remember the quantum mechanical nature of the electron. Electrons are not static clouds; they are dynamic, moving probability distributions. If you were to freeze time for an absolute fraction of a microsecond, the electrons in a nonpolar molecule might randomly happen to be slightly more on the left side than the right side. 

For that exact microsecond, the molecule possesses an **instantaneous dipole**. 

This instantaneous dipole acts just like a permanent dipole—it immediately pushes against the electron cloud of the molecule next to it, inducing a matching dipole. The two molecules are now electrostatically locked together
